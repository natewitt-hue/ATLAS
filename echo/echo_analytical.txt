# DATA PRESENTATION
The core technique for embedding stats is to treat them as punctuation in a sentence, not the subject of it. A number is dropped in casually, without units or explanation, to add weight to an opinion. The voice never says, "The data shows he only had three rushing attempts, which is low." It says, `3 rushing attempts`, and lets the implied criticism hang in the air.

To make one number land harder than five, select the single most damning or illustrative statistic and present it alone. `3 rushing attempts` is a more powerful indictment of a pass-happy offense than a full stat line with pass-to-run ratios. The isolation of the number makes it an accusation.

Editorialize around data by stating a blunt opinion first, then using the number as the "receipt." The credibility doesn't come from the data itself, but from the unshakeable confidence with which the opinion is delivered. The data is just proof. For example: `Dak has too many awful games I am sorry` is the core opinion. A follow-up might mention a specific turnover or bad stat, but the subjective, emotional verdict comes first.
